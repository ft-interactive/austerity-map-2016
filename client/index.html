Austerity update

by Sarah Neville, Bob Haslett, Martin Stabe, Aleksandra Wisniewska and John Burn-Murdoch
  • Twitter
  • Facebook
  • LinkedIn

A fresh round of welfare cuts, due to take effect next month, will widen the economic divide between the UK's highest and lowest performing economies, according to research given to the FT

Analysis shows that a raft of changes that will be implemented next month will take almost £13bn a year from claimants by 2020/21

The impact will be uneven, however, with older industrial areas, less prosperous seaside towns and some London boroughs hit hard while parts of the country, notably the south east, remain almost untouched

Larger families, in particular, will be affected, the research suggests. 15 of the 20 hardest hit places have an above average share of households with three or more children. In 12 of these 20 places the percentage of people of Asian origin exceeds 10 per cent.

Another group hard hit by the changes are working age tenants in the social rented sector. On average they can expect to lose almost £1,700 a year, compared to £290 a year for working age people who own their own home.

Blackpool and Blackburn, both in Lancashire, each lose £560 per working age adult as a result of the post-2015 reforms, compared to £150 in Guildford in Surrey, £140 in Richmond and £130 in the Hart district in Hampshire

Three years ago the FT commissioned work from Sheffield Hallam University to examine the impact of an initial round of welfare cuts. Based on Treasury assessments of the likely impact, it similarly pointed to a widening gulf between north and south

However, while the plans unveiled by George Osborne, chancellor, had called for more than £18bn to be removed from welfare budgets, the actual figure was more than £4bn less, the analysis points out

The undershoot reflects the government's failure to get nearly as many off the disability rolls as it had hoped and its over-estimate of the sums that would be saved by both the benefit cap and the spare room subsidy, known as the bedroom tax. The decision to uprate benefits by only one per cent also saved far less than anticipated due to very low inflation.
