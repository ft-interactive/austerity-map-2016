Impact of welfare reforms on Islington

A fresh round of welfare cuts, expected next month, could take £51m a year the spending power of Islington residents between now and 2020-21.

Working age claimants will lose an average of £300 a year from the benefit payout, which is likely to reduce consumer spending in the borough.

Researchers at Sheffield Hallam University measured the effect of the welfare cuts on individual local authorities and their caseload of claimants. Prof Steve Fothergill had previously estimated cuts from 2010 through 2015 for the FT. The new research assess cuts over the next five years, from April 2016 to 2020-21.

The impact on residents of Islington is moderate and broadly in line with the national trend.

Over the next five years, benefits for working age claimants there will shrink by an average of £300 a year. That means an individual will have lost an average of £690 every year in benefit payments in the decade beginning in 2010. The median impact for benefit cuts in the UK as a whole equals £300.

A freeze on working-age benefits - recently extended to 2020-21 - is the reform with the widest impact in the borough. It will cut an average of £95 per adult from the income of 28,000 households in Islington and reduce their purchasing power by XXXX?. Families with children will experience the most significant losses.

Overall, tenants in council houses will lose the most in Islington. Incomes there are expected to decrease by £1,580 a year on average, slightly below the national figure of £1,690.

Loss of purchasing power is expected to contribute to pressure on local economies, the report said. Researchers estimated London could lose 30,000 jobs in its local retail sector, more than any other region in the UK, because of lower consumer spending.
